Are Poisonous Plants an Unlikely Ally in Medicine?

They always have been for a thousand years.

 A Gympie-Gympie, credited as the most painful plant in the world. Image credit: Hugh Spencer

Poisonous plants have been regarded with fear and have been associated with pain and suffering if one is inflicted. But, these plants have been linked to beneficial uses for us Humans. Stemming from painkillers derived from the highly poisonous and addictive opium (Futterman). The ancient Greeks widely knew the Hemlock, the Hemlock serves as a sedative and as an antispasmodic. In general, many dangerous and poisonous plants can serve as medicine with broad uses that treat many problems we humans come across in our lives. (Hurst)

 

Many medicines are derived from herbs and poisonous plants, which shows that many derive from extensive use over thousands of years of human history. Poisonous plants are a vital part of the world today, even if deadly, they are necessary to create remedies and cure many deadly diseases that would kill many more than a single plant would. (Gannon)

The Hemlocks and their Poisonous Relatives

The Hemlocks, specifically the water and poison Hemlocks are very dangerous plants in which a person could die in as little as fifteen minutes if poisoned by the plant. But, for many years both have had medicinal benefits. The Poison Hemlock is used to treat muscle spasms (cramps), also the Poison Hemlock can be used as a sedative, lastly, the Poison Hemlock can be applied to tumors to treat and get rid of them (“Can Poison Hemlock Be Deadly?”). There is an even more deadly version of the Poison Hemlock, which is acclaimed to be the most poisonous plant in all of North America. It is the Water Hemlock. This Hemlock can be used as a treatment for migraines, for extremely bad and painful menstruation, and lastly to treat and kill worms that live in the intestines. But, the overall use of Water Hemlock is very dangerous, if the plant comes into contact with the skin, it can cause death (“WATER HEMLOCK: Overview, Uses, Side Effects, Precautions, Interactions, Dosing and Reviews”).

In the picture above, all are relatives of the Hemlocks and have varying degrees of toxicity and all have uses in medicines with all being used to treat a variety of things. For example, Giant Hogweed is a plant that causes skin irritation, but its uses in medicine are to treat inflammation  in the skin and joints. The Giant Hogweed can also be used to aid digestion such as bloating, gas, and indigestion ("Heracleum Mantegazzianum." Plants For A Future). Another example would be the Cow Parsnip, which also causes slight irritation on the skin. Its medical uses are linked to tonics for cold sores and sore throats (Viereck). Lastly, the Wild Parsnip also causes a case of skin irritation. One of its medical uses is in its root. The Wild Parsnip’s root contains a substance that treats psoriasis and vitiligo (“Pastinaca sativa Parsnip, Wild Parsnip PFAF Plant Database '').

The Highly Addictive Opiates/Opioids

Opioids are a narcotic derived from the poppy plant, and they can be addictive and life-threatening if consumed in dosages. Opioid/Opiate drugs are used to treat pain, and all have similar chemical properties that have that addictiveness property within them.  One drug derived from the poppy seed is Morphine. Morphine is a drug used to treat extreme pain but is a highly addictive drug that should be used as prescribed by a doctor. If used in high amounts, Morphine has the potential to cause Respiratory failure, cardiac arrest, and death (Cornell). Heroin is a narcotic derived from Morphine and is a highly dangerous and addictive substance. The side effects of Heroin are that of Morphine but amplified to an extreme degree and many times worse. Heroin is a widespread narcotic that kills very many each year and is widespread across the world. (“Heroin DrugFacts”).

The Deadly Nightshade, Foxglove, and Pacific Yew Tree

The Deadly Nightshade has been cultivated for thousands of years and has a long history of medicinal use. In the early 1500s, the Deadly Nightshade was used to put patients to sleep. In modern times, the Deadly Nightshade has a wide variety of treatments. Some of these treatments are Motion sickness, Tremors associated with Parkinson’s disease, Irritable bowel syndrome, Spastic colon, Ulcers, Diverticulitis, Conjunctivitis, Excessive urination, and  Cold and flu symptoms (Evans). Atropine is a medicine derived from the Deadly Nightshade and is used to treat low heart rate or as an antidote for cholinergic drugs or mushrooms (“Atropine (Atropine): Uses, Dosage, Side Effects, Interactions, Warning”). Digitalis is a drug that derives from the highly poisonous Foxglove plant, Digitalis is used to treat heart failure and other cardiovascular-related problems (“Digitalis Uses, Benefits & Dosage”). Even though Foxglove or Digitalis is used to treat Heart problems, if ingested by itself, the plant can cause irregular heart function and death in some cases. The Pacific Yew Tree itself is not poisonous, except for its bark. The bark contains a highly poisonous substance called Taxine, which is a very toxic part of the tree. The bark is used to produce a substance called Taxol, which is used to treat many cancers. Taxol is a very important drug that has saved many lives and is an essential part of cancer treatment and chemotherapy. The hair loss associated with chemotherapy comes from Taxol (Truxall).

Closing

Poisonous plants may have killed some, but have saved many more in their medical treatments. Poisonous plants are indeed an ally to us humans and have proved their use with their many drugs and uses. But, still be wary of the side effects, as some of these drugs can still kill you if taken in high amounts.
